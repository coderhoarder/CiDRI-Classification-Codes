Climate Mobility Framework

Classification Logic, Decision Trees, and Critical Indicators Guide
Document Purpose

This guide provides the complete classification methodology for the Climate Mobility Framework (CMF), documenting the step-by-step process for classifying households and settlements into 15 distinct mobility states based on 18 indicators across 4 pillars.

Intended Users: Researchers, policymakers, practitioners, and field teams conducting climate mobility assessments.

1
THE 5-STEP CLASSIFICATION PROCESS
CRITICAL PRINCIPLE: Classification is not purely algorithmic. While quantitative thresholds guide the process, qualitative assessment and contextual understanding are essential, especially in transitional or overlapping cases. The framework prioritizes diagnostic accuracy over mechanical scoring.
1
Assess All 18 Indicators

Score each indicator on a 1-5 scale based on detailed rubrics covering four pillars:

  • Pillar A (Physical Infrastructure): Tenure Security, Housing Quality, Infrastructure Access, Utilities, Service Access
  • Pillar B (Economic Factors): Livelihood Stability, Economic Opportunities, Financial Resources, Self-Investment
  • Pillar C (Environmental Exposure): Environmental Risk, Hazard Frequency, Resource Depletion, Ecosystem Health, Adaptive Capacity
  • Pillar D (Institutional Support): Social Networks, Community Cohesion, State Assistance, Regulatory Access

Key Principle: Use the full range of scores (1-5). Extreme scores (1-2, 4-5) are often the most diagnostic.

2
Calculate Pillar Averages

Compute the arithmetic mean for each pillar and interpret using threshold ranges:

  • <2.0 = Critical/Severe constraint
  • 2.0-2.9 = Significant challenges
  • 3.0-3.4 = Moderate/Mixed conditions
  • 3.5-4.0 = Fair/Generally functional
  • >4.0 = Good/Strong capacity
3
Identify Critical Indicator Patterns

Four indicators have special diagnostic significance:

#1 Tenure Security ANCHOR
Foundation for all investment decisions. Without secure tenure (≤2), households won't invest regardless of other conditions.
#9 Self-Investment SIGNAL
Primary commitment signal. Low scores (≤2) indicate mobility readiness; high scores (≥4) indicate settlement commitment.
#10 Environmental Risk TRIGGER
Crisis trigger. Extreme risk (≤2) can force mobility even with high investment; low risk (≥4) enables stability.
#18 Regulatory Access GATE
Access gate to formal systems. Without this (≤2), households locked out of programs, credit, legal recourse.
4
Match Pattern to Classification State

Follow the decision tree logic to identify the primary classification. The process typically follows this hierarchy:

  1. Check for extreme environmental exposure + low investment → Active Mobility States (A1-A3)
  2. Check for high investment → Immobility States (B1-B4)
  3. Check for environmental/economic drivers without investment → Active Mobility States (A4-A8)
  4. Check for mid-range scores across dimensions → Transitional States (C1-C3)
5
Diagnose Root Causes and Binding Constraints

After classification, analyze:

  • Binding Constraints: Indicators scoring ≤2 that are blocking positive outcomes
  • Critical Indicators: How the four key indicators explain the classification
  • Contributing Factors: Other indicators reinforcing the primary pattern
  • Alternative Classifications: Other plausible states given indicator variance
2
DETAILED STATE CLASSIFICATIONS

Organization: The 15 mobility states are organized into three categories:

  • Category A: Active Mobility (8 states) - Households that are mobile, preparing to move, or recently displaced
  • Category B: Immobility (4 states) - Households that are settled and not mobile
  • Category C: Transitional (3 states) - Households in unstable equilibrium that will tip toward mobility or immobility

Category A: Active Mobility States

A1: Distress Migration
Active Mobility

Critical Indicator Thresholds

#9 Self-Investment: ≤ 2
Low investment despite having been present
#10 Environmental Risk: ≤ 2
Extreme or high environmental threat
#1 Tenure Security: ≥ 3
Has achieved some documentation
#18 Regulatory Access: ≥ 3
Can access formal systems

Classification Logic

IF (Self-Investment ≤ 2) AND (Environmental Risk ≤ 2) AND (Tenure Security ≥ 3) AND (Regulatory Access ≥ 3) THEN → A1: Distress Migration

The paradox of low investment DESPITE having secure tenure and regulatory access signals that environmental threat is driving the mobility decision. This is a CRITICAL INTERVENTION WINDOW - household has documentation needed for planned relocation but environmental pressure is forcing premature departure.

Distinguishing Features

  • Unlike A3 (Crisis-Induced Displacement), A1 households have formal documentation
  • Unlike A6 (Preventive Migration), investment is minimal rather than moderate
  • Unlike B2 (Risky Persistence), low investment shows recognition that staying is untenable
Potential Overlaps & Edge Cases

→ A3 (Crisis-Induced Displacement): If tenure (score ~3) or regulatory access (score ~3) are marginally achieved and functionally weaker than documented

→ C2 (Consolidation Phase): If environmental risk is episodic (score fluctuates 2-3) rather than chronic; household might stabilize between events

A2: Trapped Population
Active Mobility - CRITICAL

Critical Indicator Thresholds

#9 Self-Investment: ≤ 2
Minimal investment recognizing futility
#10 Environmental Risk: ≤ 2
Extreme existential environmental threat
#8 Financial Resources: = 1
Zero savings/assets - THE binding constraint

Classification Logic

IF (Self-Investment ≤ 2) AND (Environmental Risk ≤ 2) AND (Financial Resources = 1) THEN → A2: Trapped Population

This is the MOST CRITICAL state - household faces extreme environmental risk but has ZERO financial capacity to relocate. They cannot afford to adapt in place (no investment resources) OR move to safety (no migration resources). This is the definition of "trapped populations" - facing imminent threat but locked in place by poverty. Requires immediate emergency intervention.

Distinguishing Features

  • Zero financial resources (score = 1) is the critical distinguisher from A1 and A3
  • Unlike A1: Has zero financial resources vs. A1 which has some capacity
  • Unlike B3: B3 has invested despite constraints; A2 cannot invest at all
Critical Warning

A2 is the highest priority for intervention. Without external support, these households face catastrophic outcomes - they will remain until forced displacement under worst possible conditions (injury, death, complete asset loss). Standard relocation programs requiring household contributions are inaccessible to A2 households.

3
SYSTEMATIC OVERLAP ANALYSIS
UNDERSTANDING OVERLAPS: Overlaps between mobility states are not errors or ambiguities in the framework - they reflect real-world complexity where households exhibit characteristics of multiple states simultaneously or are in transition between states.

Major Overlap Zones

Environmental Crisis Cluster (A1/A2/A3)

Common Ground: All three share extreme environmental risk (≤2) + low investment (≤2)

Distinguishing Dimensions:

  • Financial Capacity: A2 (zero) vs. A1/A3 (some)
  • Formal Access: A1 (has) vs. A3 (lacks) vs. A2 (irrelevant)
Economic Mobility Cluster (A4/A5)

Common Ground: Low investment (≤2) + safe environment (≥4)

Distinguishing Dimension:

  • Economic standing: A4 (Pillar B ≤2.0) vs. A5 (Pillar B >2.0)
  • Desperation vs. choice-driven mobility
Vulnerable Immobility Cluster (B2/B3)

Common Ground: High investment (≥4) creating immobility despite vulnerabilities

Distinguishing Dimensions:

  • B2: Environmental risk + capacity to leave but chooses to stay
  • B3: Multiple constraints + trapped by sunk costs
Transitional Cluster (C1/C2/C3)

Common Ground: Mid-range tenure (2-3) + mid-range investment (2-3)

Distinguishing Dimensions:

  • C1: Favorable environment (≥4) - positive trajectory
  • C2: Environmental stress (≤2) - critical window
  • C3: Balanced scores - ambiguous direction
4
CONFIDENCE SCORING METHODOLOGY

Every classification should be assigned a confidence score (0-100%) indicating how clearly the indicator pattern matches the assigned state.

Base Confidence Assignment

80% Base
Pattern matches decision tree clearly with all critical indicators solidly meeting thresholds (e.g., scores of 1 when threshold is ≤2)
65% Base
Pattern matches but some critical indicators are borderline (within 0.5 points of threshold)
50% Base
Pattern is ambiguous, requiring assumptions about unmeasured factors or interpretation of contradictory indicators

Confidence Adjustments

Increase confidence (+5 to +15%): Multiple critical indicators strongly align, pillar averages reinforce classification, no significant contradictory indicators, supporting qualitative evidence corroborates

Decrease confidence (-5 to -20%): Critical indicators at exact thresholds, mixed signals across dimensions, multiple plausible alternative classifications, pillar averages don't support primary classification

Interpretation Guidelines

Confidence Range Interpretation
85-100% High confidence - Clear classification, minimal alternative explanations
70-84% Good confidence - Strong primary classification with minor alternatives
55-69% Moderate confidence - Primary classification likely but alternatives viable
40-54% Low confidence - Multiple interpretations equally plausible, requires qualitative investigation
<40% Very low confidence - Classification is best guess, high uncertainty
